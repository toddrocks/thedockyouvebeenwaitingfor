Underneath the numbers

Where the value actually sits.

01

Frontage, and the kind of water it fronts

130 feet of open, south-facing Choctawhatchee Bay — more frontage than four of the five comps. One comp sold at $1,125,000 on 75 feet of bayou; another is pending at $950,000 on 80 feet. Not all waterfront is the same product, and open bay sits at the top of that hierarchy.

02

The dock is capital already in the ground

A 120-foot dock running to a boat lift, with fish-cleaning station, shower, and power, represents roughly $175,000–$250,000 to build today — and permitting timelines on the north shore mean it isn't something a buyer can simply add later. The two comps without docks are the two weakest performers per waterfront foot in the set.

03

Seawall and rip rap are baseline, not bonus

Shoreline protection is a requirement at this price point, not a luxury — and replacing it is a five-figure project. Most of the comps carry bulkhead or seawall. This property has vinyl seawall and rip rap already in place.

04

Land and structures

At 0.90 acres this is the second-largest parcel in the set, with a detached 2-car garage, carport, and a separate storage building and workshop. Only one comp offers more land — and it offers none of these structures.

05

Carrying costs

No HOA and no assessment, with 2025 taxes of $800.90. Two comps in the set carry a $600 annual assessment plus taxes of $5,823 and $6,737. For a buyer running the monthly numbers, that difference compounds every year of ownership.

Fair questions

The four objections, answered straight.

Every property has them. Here are this one's, with the data rather than a deflection.

The house needs a full renovation.

True, and it's already priced in. The adjustment grid carries the condition gap at roughly $200,000 — it is not deducted twice. All in at $1,200,000, this property still out-measures the $1,200,000 comp on water, land, size, dock and outbuildings.

Flood insurance runs about $3,000 a year.

Real, and disclosed up front. It's a carrying cost, not a value destroyer — and it's partly offset by a tax bill of $800.90 against comps paying $5,823 and $6,737 with an HOA assessment on top.

Only two bathrooms.

A legitimate functional limitation. It's also one of the highest-return line items inside the renovation budget — and the floor plan, with a 26×26 family room and vaulted ceilings, gives you room to solve it.

It was built in 1968.

All brick, vaulted ceilings, brick fireplace, windows on three sides of the family room. The bones are exactly why the renovation math works. A comparable brick home on this same road established that the market pays over $1,000,000 for this profile — with no dock at all.
